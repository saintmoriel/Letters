Dear Mr. Thomas Baido,

I have been sitting with this for a while, trying to find the right way to say what I want to say. And I think the only honest way is to just say it plainly.

You are the most hardworking lecturer I have encountered at Veritas. The most brilliant. A mathematician, a networking engineer, someone who showed up to every class like the subject itself depended on him. I noticed that. I always noticed that.

But here is what you do not know.

From early on, I convinced myself that you did not like me. Not because of anything you said directly, but because of the people around you. The sharp ones, the fast ones, the ones who seemed to speak your language naturally. I looked at them, and then I looked at myself, and I made a decision quietly: he will never see me the way he sees them. So I pulled back.

I stopped coming to your office as I should have — even as NACOS President. I stopped trying the way I should have. There were times I deliberately underperformed, not out of laziness, but out of self-protection. If I never really tried, then it would not hurt that you did not notice.

I even said it out loud to someone once. "I don't think Mr. Thomas likes me."

I am not telling you this to make you feel responsible. I am telling you because it is true, and because this letter would mean nothing if I left that part out.

What I carried from you was not always comfortable. But it was real. You showed me what standard looks like — what it looks like when someone refuses to be ordinary at what they do. And even from a distance, even from behind the wall I had built, that got into me. It is in me now.

You will teach hundreds more students after me. My face will fade. That is just the nature of it, and I hold no grief about that. But you will not fade from me. The way you showed up, the way you commanded your craft, the way you made me feel……all of it……stays.

I am grateful, sir. Not despite the complexity of what I felt, but including it. I am grateful I got to sit in your class. I am grateful you passed me when maybe the grade alone did not justify it — when I didn't even deserve it. I am grateful for the privilege of walking this particular path, even the parts that were hard, even the parts that were my own doing.

This is my goodbye. And it is a full one.

With gratitude,
